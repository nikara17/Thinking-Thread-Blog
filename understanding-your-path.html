Understanding Your Path: University, Certifications, and Self-Taught Learning

How people actually enter technology careers in the modern world.

2026 • Nikara Ishwarlall • 10 min read

For many people, the world of technology feels like a closed room — a place you can only enter through one specific doorway: a university degree. If you didn’t study computer science, engineering, or information technology, it can feel like that door has already closed.

This belief keeps countless capable people from even attempting to enter the field. High school students feel pressured to make the “right” decision immediately. University students worry they may have chosen the wrong path. Adults already working in other industries often believe it is too late to change direction.

But the reality is far more flexible than most people realise. Technology careers do not follow a single road. In fact, there are multiple doorways into the industry — and many of them remain invisible to people who have never been exposed to the ecosystem.

The three major paths into technology

Most people enter the technology industry through one of three broad routes:

  • University education
  • Professional certifications
  • Self-directed learning

These paths are not mutually exclusive. Many professionals combine elements of all three throughout their careers. The key difference is simply where someone begins building their skills.

The university path

University remains the most traditional route into technology. Degrees in computer science, information technology, software engineering, and robotics provide structured academic training and exposure to fundamental theory.

These environments often offer access to research labs, mentorship from lecturers, and collaborative projects that help students build deeper understanding of complex systems.

However, university is not the only doorway. While it can provide a strong theoretical foundation, it is only one of several paths into the technology ecosystem.

The certification path

Professional certifications focus on practical skills used in real industry environments. Instead of studying broad theory, learners often train on specific tools or platforms used by companies.

Examples include networking infrastructure, cybersecurity practices, and cloud computing platforms. Certifications can allow learners to specialise quickly and often complement existing work experience.

Many professionals pursue certifications while working, gradually building new skills alongside their existing responsibilities.

The self-taught path

The internet has dramatically expanded access to technical learning. Today, thousands of tutorials, online courses, documentation libraries, and open-source projects allow people to develop technology skills independently.

Self-directed learners often gain experience by building projects, experimenting with tools, and solving real problems. While this path requires discipline and persistence, it offers enormous flexibility for individuals who may not have access to formal education.

The modern reality: careers evolve

Technology careers rarely follow straight lines. Someone may begin with a university degree, later pursue certifications, and continue learning through independent projects. Others may start self-taught and eventually enter formal education.

What matters most is the ability to keep learning and adapting as technology evolves.

Why many people feel trapped

The biggest barrier facing aspiring technologists is often not ability but visibility. People cannot walk through doorways they do not know exist.

If someone grows up believing that technology careers are only for computer science graduates or elite programmers, they may never explore other possibilities.

Looking forward

Automation, artificial intelligence, and digital systems are transforming industries across the world. As technology continues to reshape the global workforce, new opportunities are emerging for people willing to learn and adapt.

Technology careers are no longer defined by a single educational path. Multiple doorways exist for people willing to explore them.

If you feel uncertain about your future, remember this: you are not locked into one decision made years ago. You are simply standing in a hallway with more doors than you may have realised.

Understanding the paths is only the first step. The next question is the one most people actually need answered: how do you make those paths financially possible?

Many opportunities exist that students and career-changers rarely hear about — including bursaries, internships, sponsored certifications, employer-supported learning, and free platforms that make self-directed growth more realistic.
